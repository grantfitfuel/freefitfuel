Anxiety

An alarm system trying to protect you. We can train it to ring less — and ring wiser.

What anxiety is, how it works in the body, and practical tools to steady the system — from breathing and grounding to exposure ladders and reframing. UK support links included.

1) What anxiety feels like

Racing thoughts, tight chest, stomach upset, sweating, shakiness, restlessness, or feeling detached. It often pops up in ordinary places: mornings, emails, queues, bedtime.

Do now (30s): Name 1 sensation (e.g., “tight chest”) and 1 place you feel it most. Noting it reduces the “unknown” factor.

Anxiety often shows in everyday moments, not just major crises.

2) Biology — how it happens

  • Sympathetic system: fight/flight — adrenaline speeds heart/breath to prepare for action.
  • Parasympathetic (vagal) brake: long exhales, humming, and social safety cues help it engage.
  • Neurochemistry: serotonin (mood), GABA (calm), noradrenaline (alert), dopamine (drive).
  • Cortisol: useful in bursts; when stuck “on” it keeps the body keyed up.
  • CO₂ balance: slow nasal breathing with longer exhales reduces alarm signals.

The autonomic system shifts between alert, calm, and shutdown. We can nudge it back toward calm.

3) Day-to-day tools

  • Breathing: inhale 4, exhale 6–8, through the nose if you can. Soften shoulders and jaw.
  • Grounding 5-4-3-2-1: 5 see • 4 feel • 3 hear • 2 smell • 1 taste.
  • Movement: 2–10 minutes of walk, stretch, or shaking out arms to burn adrenaline.
  • Temperature: cool water on wrists/face for 30–60s; or warmth (blanket, warm mug) for comfort.
  • Light & timing: morning daylight helps the system settle across the day.
Do now (60s): 6 slow breaths with a longer out-breath. Notice one area that eases.

Tiny, repeatable rituals teach the alarm that you’re safe.

4) Exposure ladders

Avoidance strengthens fear; gradual approach retrains it. Build a ladder from easy to hard and climb at a doable pace.

  1. List 6–8 steps from easy → hard (e.g., “read the email subject” → “reply to the email”).
  2. Practise a low step for several reps until anxiety drops by ~30–50%.
  3. Climb one rung; step back if needed; repeat. Pair with the breathing above.

Retrain the alarm, one gentle rung at a time.

5) Thinking tools

Supportive self-talk

Swap harsh inner lines for kinder, believable ones. “This is uncomfortable, and I can take one small step.”

Kind, specific language supports calming circuits.

Reframes

Keep the facts, add choice and agency. “I don’t need to solve everything tonight; I’ll pick one 5-minute action.”

Accurate, balanced reframes stop spirals without false positivity.
