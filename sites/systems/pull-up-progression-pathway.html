Pull-Up Progression Pathway

A fully coached pull-up progression system focused on hanging confidence, scapular control, lat activation, assisted work, rows, negatives and sustainable full pull-up development.

Strength & skill All levels 2–3 times weekly Scalable progression

What This System Is For

Many people struggle with pull-ups because they jump straight to full reps before building the pieces that make the movement work: grip tolerance, shoulder blade control, lat engagement, body tension and controlled lowering. This pathway breaks the skill into stages so the user can progress without relying on ugly reps or repeated failure.

Use the pain rule: tired muscles are fine, but sharp elbow pain, shoulder pinching, numbness, worsening biceps tendon pain or loss of control means stop and regress.

  • Grip numbness appears
  • Biceps tendon pain increases
  • You cannot control the lowering phase

How To Use This Pathway

Beginner

Start with dead hangs, scapular pulls, band lat activation and inverted rows. Do not test full pull-ups every session.

Intermediate

Use assisted pull-ups, negatives and cluster work. Keep reps clean and stop before form collapses.

Progression Rule

Progress when you can complete all sets with controlled shoulders, no swinging and no next-day tendon flare.

Phase 1 — Scapular Control & Hanging Confidence

Dead Hangs

3 sets × 15–30 seconds

Purpose

Builds grip confidence, shoulder tolerance and familiarity with hanging before harder pulling work.

Starting Position
  • Use a pull-up bar you can reach safely.
  • Grip overhand or neutral if available.
  • Start with feet lightly supported if needed.
How To Do It
  • Hang with long arms.
  • Keep shoulders controlled, not jammed into the ears.
  • Breathe slowly.
  • Step down before grip fully fails.
What You Should Feel

Grip, forearms and gentle shoulder loading. You should not feel sharp shoulder pinching or nerve-like symptoms.

Common Mistakes
  • Dropping into a painful dead hang.
  • Holding until complete grip failure.
  • Swinging aggressively.
  • Ignoring elbow or shoulder pain.
Easier / Harder

Easier: keep toes on the floor or use a box.
Harder: add time slowly before adding harder variations.

Scapular Pulls

3 sets × 6–10 reps

Purpose

Teaches the shoulder blades to move down and back before the arms bend. This is the first layer of a strong pull-up.

Starting Position
  • Hang from the bar with arms mostly straight.
  • Use foot support if full hanging is too much.
  • Keep ribs controlled.
How To Do It
  • Without bending elbows much, pull shoulder blades down.
  • Lift the chest slightly towards the bar.
  • Pause briefly.
  • Return slowly to the start.
What You Should Feel

Lower shoulder blade and lat engagement. Not a biceps curl or neck shrug.

Common Mistakes
  • Bending elbows too much.
  • Shrugging upwards.
  • Swinging.
  • Trying to pull too high too soon.
Easier / Harder

Easier: do it with feet supported.
Harder: add a 2-second pause at the top.

Band Lat Activation

3 sets × 12–15 reps

Purpose

Helps the user feel the lats working before trying to pull bodyweight from the bar.

Starting Position
  • Anchor a light band overhead or in front.
  • Hold the band with arms extended.
  • Stand tall with ribs down.
How To Do It
  • Drive elbows towards the ribs.
  • Pause briefly at the bottom.
  • Return slowly.
  • Keep shoulders away from the ears.
What You Should Feel

Muscular work down the sides of the back. Not neck tension or elbow strain.

Common Mistakes
  • Using too much band resistance.
  • Arching the back.
  • Pulling with wrists only.
  • Letting shoulders roll forwards.
Easier / Harder

Easier: use a lighter band.
Harder: add a slower return phase.

Phase 2 — Assisted Pull-Up Strength

Band-Assisted Pull-Ups

4 sets × 4–8 reps

Purpose

Lets the user practise the full pull-up pattern with enough help to keep form clean.

Starting Position
  • Loop a band safely around the bar.
  • Place foot or knee into the band carefully.
  • Start from a controlled hang.
How To Do It
  • Set the shoulder blades first.
  • Pull elbows towards the ribs.
  • Aim chest towards the bar.
  • Lower under control.
What You Should Feel

Back and arm effort with smooth control. The band should support the movement, not launch you.

Common Mistakes
  • Using too little assistance too early.
  • Kicking or swinging.
  • Stopping halfway down.
  • Chasing reps after form breaks.
Easier / Harder

Easier: use a stronger band.
Harder: use less band support or slow the lowering phase.

Feet-Assisted Pull-Ups

3 sets × 6–10 reps

Purpose

Allows very precise assistance because the legs can help only as much as needed.

Starting Position
  • Set a box, bench or floor position under the bar.
  • Keep feet lightly supported.
  • Grip the bar securely.
How To Do It
  • Pull with the upper body first.
  • Use legs only enough to complete the rep.
  • Pause near the top.
  • Lower slowly.
Common Mistakes
  • Turning it into a leg press.
  • Using no control on the way down.
  • Letting shoulders collapse at the bottom.
  • Rushing reps.
Easier / Harder

Easier: use more leg support.
Harder: reduce leg help or add a pause.

Inverted Rows

3 sets × 8–12 reps

Purpose

Builds pulling strength with a friendlier body angle than full pull-ups.

Starting Position
  • Use rings, a bar, or a sturdy row setup.
  • Body straight from shoulders to heels if possible.
  • Start with arms extended.
How To Do It
  • Pull chest towards the handle or bar.
  • Drive elbows backwards.
  • Pause briefly.
  • Lower slowly.
What You Should Feel

Upper back, lats and arms. Keep ribs and hips from sagging.

Common Mistakes
  • Hips dropping.
  • Neck reaching forwards.
  • Half reps.
  • Letting shoulders roll forwards.
Easier / Harder

Easier: stand more upright.
Harder: walk feet forwards to make the body more horizontal.

Phase 3 — Negative Strength & Full Pull-Ups

Negative Pull-Ups

3 sets × 3–5 reps

Purpose

Builds the strength needed to control the lowering phase, one of the strongest bridges to the first full pull-up.

Starting Position
  • Use a box to start at the top.
  • Chin near or over the bar.
  • Shoulders controlled, not shrugged hard.
How To Do It
  • Step off the box carefully.
  • Lower over 3–5 seconds.
  • Keep elbows tracking under control.
  • Step back to the box and reset.
Common Mistakes
  • Dropping too fast.
  • Doing too many reps.
  • Starting with poor top position.
  • Letting elbows or shoulders flare painfully.
Easier / Harder

Easier: use band or foot support.
Harder: extend the lowering to 5 seconds.

Single Pull-Up Attempts

3–5 quality attempts

Purpose

Practises the real skill without turning the session into repeated failure.

How To Do It
  • Attempt one clean rep.
  • Rest properly afterwards.
  • Stop before ugly grinding.
  • Use assistance if the first rep is not close.
Quality Standard

Controlled start, no violent kicking, chest rises, elbows drive down, and lowering is not a collapse.

Common Mistakes
  • Testing max attempts every session.
  • Turning every rep into a fight.
  • Swinging for momentum.
  • Ignoring tendon pain.

Pull-Up Clusters

Multiple low-rep sets

Purpose

Builds volume through clean reps instead of one exhausted high-rep set.

How To Do It
  • Do 1–3 clean reps.
  • Rest until quality returns.
  • Repeat several small sets.
  • Stop when form drops.
Progression Rule

Add total clean reps across the session before chasing bigger sets.

Common Mistakes
  • Taking every cluster to failure.
  • Shortening rest too much.
  • Losing shoulder position.
  • Calling partial reps full reps.

Technique Principles

Helpful Cues

  • Set shoulders before bending the elbows.
  • Pull elbows towards the ribs.
  • Keep ribs controlled and body quiet.
  • Lower with the same discipline you used to pull.

Common Errors

  • Jerking violently from the bottom.
  • Collapsing shoulders.
  • Excessive swinging.
  • Ignoring elbow or biceps pain signals.
