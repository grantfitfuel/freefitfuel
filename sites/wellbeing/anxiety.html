Anxiety

An alarm system trying to protect you. We can train it to ring less — and ring wiser.

This page explains what anxiety is, how it works, and practical tools to steady the system.

1) What Anxiety Feels Like

Anxiety can show up as racing thoughts, tight chest, stomach upset, sweating, shakiness, or feeling detached. It often arrives in ordinary places: mornings, emails, queues, bedtime.

Quick grounding (30–60s)
  1. 5-4-3-2-1: name 5 things you see, 4 touch, 3 hear, 2 smell, 1 taste.
  2. Breathe slow: in for 4, out for 6 (longer exhale).
  3. Find warmth: hands around a mug, blanket, or warm water over hands.

Anxiety often shows in everyday routines, not just major crises.

2) Biology / How It Happens

  • Sympathetic system: fight/flight response and adrenaline.
  • Neurotransmitters: serotonin (mood), GABA (calm), norepinephrine (alert), dopamine (motivation).
  • Cortisol: useful in short bursts; unhelpful when stuck “on”.
  • Breathing & CO₂: fast, shallow breathing raises alarm; slow exhale dampens it.
Reduce daily cortisol “drip”
  • Morning light 5–10 min; late-day lights dim.
  • Batch tasks; take 3–5 min breath breaks each hour.
  • Stop caffeine 6–8h before bed; keep evenings quieter.

Your nervous system moves between alert, calm, and shutdown states.

3) Day-to-Day Coping Tools

  • Grounding 5-4-3-2-1 (see above).
  • Breathing: in 4, out 6 — or coherent 5/5 for 5–7 minutes.
  • Movement: walk, stretch, or shake arms to burn off adrenaline.
  • Warmth: blanket, warm hands, warm shower.
Calm sounds Breath guides

Small rituals — breath, grounding, warmth — calm the alarm.

4) Exposure Ladders

Avoidance strengthens fear. Exposure ladders mean facing the fear gradually, step by step.

  1. List situations from easiest → hardest.
  2. Start with a low step until anxiety eases.
  3. Climb slowly; repeat steps as needed.
Build a gentle ladder
  • Pick 1 theme (e.g., phone calls). Write 5–8 rungs, very small.
  • Measure with a 0–10 “anxiety now” rating before/after.
  • Repeat a rung until “after” drops by ~30–50% on two attempts.

Retrain the alarm one gentle rung at a time.

5) Thinking Tools

Supportive self-talk

Swap harsh words for kinder, believable lines.

Kind, specific language supports calming circuits.

Reframes

Keep the facts but add choice and agency. No false positivity.

Accurate, balanced reframes help stop the spiral.

Try these lines
  • “This feeling is loud, not dangerous. It will pass.”
  • “I can do one small step while breathing slowly.”
  • “Future-me can handle that email after a short walk.”
Common thinking traps
  • Catastrophising (“It will be a disaster”).
  • Mind-reading (“They think I’m useless”).
  • All-or-nothing (“If not perfect, I failed”).

Support

If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately. For non-emergencies, consider speaking with a qualified health professional.
