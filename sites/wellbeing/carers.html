Carer Support

Caring is meaningful and demanding. You deserve practical help, rest, and clear information.

This page gathers simple tools and UK links for **unpaid carers** — whether you look after a partner, child, parent, friend or neighbour. Use the mini-nav to jump to what you need.

1) Who counts as a carer?

You are a carer if you provide unpaid help to someone who couldn’t manage without it. That might be support with medicines, personal care, mobility, meals, travel, appointments, or keeping the home running.

Tip: tell your GP you’re a carer — practices can add this to your record and share local support.

Noticing that you are a carer is the first step to getting support.

2) Your wellbeing

  • Micro-rest: 3–5 short breath/move breaks across the day (set a light reminder).
  • Sleep basics: aim for a regular wind-down, dim lights, and a quick next-day list so your brain can switch off.
  • Cortisol calm: slow breathing (in 4, out 6–8), a brief outside walk, and warm hands/drink to soften the stress response.
  • Share the load: make a simple task list and invite family/friends to take one small thing regularly.
Short breathing reset (1–2 minutes)

Inhale softly through the nose for 4, exhale for 6–8. Shoulders stay heavy; jaw unclenched. Repeat ~10–12 cycles.

Quick support at home

  • Keep a meds list on the fridge and a photo on your phone.
  • Put key contacts in one place: GP, out-of-hours, pharmacy, social work, trusted neighbour.
  • Use a spare keys plan with a neighbour/friend you trust.

3) Money & rights (UK quick guide)

  • Carer’s Assessment (via your local council) — looks at your needs as a carer and what support could help.
  • Carer’s Allowance (if eligible) and related top-ups; other possible benefits include Universal Credit, Pension Credit additions.
  • Young carers have access to specific support via schools/colleges and local services.
  • Employment — talk with your employer about flexible working or carer’s leave options.

Rules differ across UK nations and can change — check the services below for up-to-date info.

4) Planning & emergencies

  • Emergency plan: who to call, what to do, meds/allergies, key routines.
  • Back-up contacts: at least two people who can step in for an hour or a day.
  • Grab-bag: copies of IDs, meds list, spare chargers, small cash, essentials.

5) Respite & breaks

Short breaks matter. Options vary by area but may include:

  • Local authority funded short breaks via carer services.
  • Voluntary sector respite schemes and sitting services.
  • Family/friends taking a regular slot each week (“every Wed 2–4pm”).

Ask your local carer centre about what’s available where you live.

6) Quick tools

  • Symptom tracker: note flare-ups, meds taken, meals, fluids, sleep, mood.
  • Shared task list: write 6–8 jobs others can choose from (shopping, garden, lifts).
  • Call script: have key lines ready for GP/social work/111 to reduce stress on the call.

De-stress micro-routine (3 minutes)

  1. Stand or sit tall. Inhale 4, exhale 6–8 for 8–10 breaths.
  2. Roll shoulders, shake out hands, stretch calves.
  3. Drink water; step outside for 60 seconds of daylight if possible.

7) Support services (UK & Scotland)

  • Carers UK — national advice, forums, rights and entitlements.
  • Carers Trust — local carer services and young carer support.
  • NHS Inform Scotland: Carers — Scottish information and getting help.
  • Your local council — Carer’s Assessment and support in your area (search “carer assessment” + your council).
  • Citizens Advice — benefits and work advice.
  • Young Carer Grant (Scotland) — if eligible.

Important Note

This page provides general information and links. It’s not a substitute for professional medical, legal or financial advice. For personalised guidance, contact your local carer service or an advice organisation above.
