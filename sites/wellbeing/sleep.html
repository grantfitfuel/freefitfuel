Sleep & Insomnia

A deep dive into how sleep works, why insomnia sticks, and the tools that actually shift things — including CBT-I, 3 am rescue steps, and guides for shift workers, teens, menopause, chronic pain, PTSD/nightmares, and sleep apnoea. UK & Scotland support links included.

14) Sleep Apnoea & CPAP

  • Red flags: loud snoring, breath pauses, gasping, morning headaches, daytime sleepiness.
  • Ask your GP about a home sleep study; CPAP is highly effective when recommended.
  • CPAP tips: try mask while reading; adjust humidity; clean regularly; work with clinic on fit.
  • Side-sleeping, healthy weight, and reduced evening alcohol help mild OSA.
  • Do not drive if excessively sleepy.

15) 3 am Emergency Toolkit

Decision tree (5–10 min)

  1. Turn clock away. Long exhale × 10.
  2. Still wired? Leave bed: dim light, quiet chair.
  3. Do one calming loop: breath/scan/reading.
  4. Brain dump or 3-bullet “tomorrow” if worries persist.
  5. Return to bed only when drowsy. Repeat as needed.

Quick resets

  • 4-7-8 breaths × 4 rounds.
  • Progressive release: hands → shoulders → jaw.
  • Warm your feet; sip water.
  • Self-talk: “Rest counts. Good-enough sleep is fine.”

Support (UK & Scotland)

  • NHS urgent mental health helplines (England, 24/7)
  • NHS Inform Scotland — mental health & sleep
  • Mind — Sleep problems
  • Samaritans — 116 123, free 24/7

Forces & Emergency Responders

  • Combat Stress — veterans’ trauma/nightmares.
  • Veterans’ Gateway — 24/7 first point of contact.
  • NHS Op COURAGE — veterans’ NHS mental health.
  • Mind — Blue Light — police, fire, ambulance.
  • Police Care UK — serving & former police and families.
  • The Fire Fighters Charity.
  • TASC — Ambulance Staff Charity.

When to seek medical help

  • Snoring + breathing pauses, gasping, morning headaches, severe daytime sleepiness.
  • Insomnia >3 months despite trying behavioural changes.
  • Restless legs/periodic limb movements disrupting sleep.
  • Nightmares/flashbacks linked to trauma not easing.
  • Mania history or bipolar disorder (get advice before sleep restriction).
  • Sleepiness impacting driving/safety — do not drive.

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical or psychological advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a qualified health professional or one of the support services listed on our site.
