Lymphatic Recovery Flow

A fully coached low-impact recovery flow focused on circulation, gentle movement, breathing, walking rhythm and reducing sedentary heaviness and stiffness.

Recovery movement Low impact Circulation support Daily use friendly

What This System Is For

The body responds well to regular low-level movement. Walking, breathing, muscular contractions and posture changes all help support circulation and recovery. This flow is designed to reduce stiffness and encourage movement without turning recovery into punishment.

This is not a “detox cure”. The goal is gentle recovery support through movement, circulation and reduced sedentary behaviour.

Useful For

  • Recovery days
  • Deskbound stiffness
  • Heavy-leg feeling
  • Low-energy movement
  • Post-training circulation

Pairs Well With

  • Daily Recovery Flow
  • Walking plans
  • Tai chi walking
  • Deskbound Reset Flow
  • Lower-leg stability work

Reduce Intensity If

  • Dizziness develops
  • Swelling worsens suddenly
  • Breathing becomes strained
  • Pain escalates sharply
  • Fatigue becomes overwhelming

How To Use This Flow

Morning Movement

Use 5–10 minutes after waking to reduce stiffness and start circulation gently.

Recovery Days

Use as a lighter movement layer instead of complete inactivity.

Deskbound Breaks

Use short movement rounds throughout the day to avoid long sedentary periods.

Phase 1 — Breathing & Gentle Activation

Breathing Reset

1–3 minutes

Purpose

Helps calm stress breathing patterns and improve movement rhythm.

How To Do It
  • Breathe slowly through the nose if comfortable.
  • Allow ribs to expand naturally.
  • Relax shoulders and jaw.
  • Exhale slowly without forcing.
What You Should Feel

Reduced upper-body tension and calmer breathing rhythm.

Common Mistakes
  • Overbreathing aggressively.
  • Trying too hard to relax.
  • Forcing huge breaths.
  • Raising shoulders excessively.
Easier / Harder

Easier: shorter rounds.
Harder: extend the exhale slightly.

Shoulder & Arm Pump Flow

1–2 minutes

Purpose

Encourages upper-body circulation and reduces stiffness from prolonged sitting.

How To Do It
  • Roll shoulders gently.
  • Swing arms loosely.
  • Open and close the chest slowly.
  • Keep movement smooth and relaxed.
What You Should Feel

Warmth and gentle circulation through shoulders and arms.

Common Mistakes
  • Forcing mobility aggressively.
  • Shrugging shoulders hard.
  • Moving too quickly.
  • Holding tension in the neck.
Easier / Harder

Easier: smaller movements.
Harder: increase movement time gradually.

Calf Pump Raises

2–3 sets × 12–20 reps

Purpose

The calves help support circulation during walking and movement. Gentle calf work helps reduce heavy-leg stiffness.

How To Do It
  • Rise gently onto the balls of the feet.
  • Pause briefly.
  • Lower slowly.
  • Keep movement controlled.
What You Should Feel

Gentle calf effort and circulation increase without strain.

Common Mistakes
  • Bouncing aggressively.
  • Holding breath.
  • Ignoring cramp.
  • Locking knees.
Easier / Harder

Easier: use wall support.
Harder: add pauses at the top.

Phase 2 — Walking & Flow

Tai Chi Walking

3–8 minutes

Purpose

Supports rhythm, circulation and calm movement quality without high impact.

How To Do It
  • Take slow deliberate steps.
  • Shift weight smoothly.
  • Relax shoulders and jaw.
  • Keep breathing calm.
What You Should Feel

Gentle movement rhythm and reduced physical tension.

Common Mistakes
  • Rushing.
  • Trying to move perfectly.
  • Holding unnecessary tension.
  • Turning it into cardio.
Easier / Harder

Easier: shorter duration.
Harder: extend the walk slowly over time.

Whole-Body Shakeout

30–60 seconds

Purpose

Helps release unnecessary muscular tension and stiffness.

How To Do It
  • Shake arms gently.
  • Loosen shoulders.
  • Let the body move lightly.
  • Keep breathing easy.
What You Should Feel

A looser lighter feeling rather than aggressive exertion.

Common Mistakes
  • Shaking violently.
  • Tensing the neck.
  • Moving too aggressively.
  • Holding breath.
Easier / Harder

Easier: smaller shake patterns.
Harder: extend duration slightly.

Hip & Ankle Mobility

1–2 minutes each area

Purpose

Supports smoother lower-body movement and reduces sedentary stiffness.

How To Do It
  • Circle hips gently.
  • Circle ankles slowly.
  • Keep knees soft.
  • Use support if balance feels uncertain.
What You Should Feel

Gentle mobility and circulation rather than stretching strain.

Common Mistakes
  • Forcing large circles.
  • Moving too fast.
  • Locking knees.
  • Ignoring balance issues.
Easier / Harder

Easier: smaller circles.
Harder: slower tempo with pauses.

Phase 3 — Recovery Downshift

Slow March Flow

2–4 minutes

Purpose

Maintains circulation while gradually lowering overall effort.

How To Do It
  • March slowly on the spot.
  • Place feet quietly.
  • Relax the shoulders.
  • Keep breathing calm.
What You Should Feel

Gentle warmth and smoother breathing rhythm.

Common Mistakes
  • Stomping heavily.
  • Turning it into cardio.
  • Rushing.
  • Tensing shoulders.

Recovery Flow Linking

2–5 minutes

Purpose

Creates smooth whole-body movement instead of isolated drills.

How To Do It
  • Choose several movements from this page.
  • Move slowly between them.
  • Keep breathing relaxed.
  • Stop before fatigue rises sharply.
What You Should Feel

Reduced stiffness and smoother movement rhythm.

Common Mistakes
  • Moving too quickly.
  • Trying to “burn calories”.
  • Forcing mobility.
  • Ignoring fatigue.

Final Calm Breathing

1–2 minutes

Purpose

Helps finish the session feeling calmer rather than overstimulated.

How To Do It
  • Breathe slowly through the nose if comfortable.
  • Relax shoulders and jaw.
  • Allow breathing to settle naturally.
  • Keep posture relaxed.
What You Should Feel

Reduced tension and calmer recovery state.

Common Mistakes
  • Forcing deep breaths.
  • Trying too hard to relax.
  • Holding tension.
  • Rushing straight back into stress.

Recovery Principles

Helpful Recovery Habits

  • Regular walking
  • Breaking up long sitting periods
  • Hydration support
  • Consistent low-pressure movement

Often Less Helpful

  • Total inactivity
  • Turning recovery into punishment
  • Ignoring fatigue signals
  • Extreme “detox” claims and routines
