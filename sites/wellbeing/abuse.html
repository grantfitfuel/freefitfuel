Abuse Support

You are not to blame and you are not alone. There are safe steps and people who can help.

This page explains what abuse can look like, why leaving is difficult, steps to build a simple safety plan, and where to find confidential support.

1) Understanding Abuse

Abuse is about power and control. It can happen in relationships, families, or institutions. It may include:

  • Physical: hitting, pushing, restraining, physical harm.
  • Emotional/Psychological: insults, threats, humiliation, intimidation, gaslighting.
  • Financial: controlling money, denying access, forced debt, dependency.
  • Coercive control: isolation, monitoring, controlling movements and contacts.
  • Sexual: any non-consensual sexual contact or pressure.

Recognising abuse is the first step. It is not your fault.

You deserve safety, dignity and peace.

2) Why it feels so hard to leave

Feeling stuck is common: fear, finances, social pressure, children, immigration status, disability, or hope that things will improve. Abuse also changes how your brain and body respond to threat — anxiety, depression, trauma responses, or shutdown. None of this means you are weak.

If leaving feels impossible now, there are still steps you can take to increase safety and gather support.

3) Steps you can take (at your pace)

  • Talk to someone you trust — friend, colleague, neighbour, or a helpline volunteer trained in abuse.
  • Make a simple safety plan — know a safe place; store key documents (photos/scans) somewhere safe; keep a small essentials bag if possible.
  • Use technology safely — private device if possible; clear history; review location sharing and “find my” settings.
  • Record incidents if safe — dates/times, photos of injuries, threatening messages. Only if it doesn’t increase risk.
Build a safety plan
  1. Identify a safe room and a code word you can text/say to allies.
  2. Gather copies of ID, passports, meds, bank info; store with a trusted person or secure cloud.
  3. Plan routes & transport (spare keys, bus routes, taxi numbers).
  4. Teach children simple cues like “go next door and call 999” if age-appropriate.
Technology safety
  • Check for tracking/spyware; update passwords; enable 2FA on a private email.
  • Turn off or limit location sharing.
  • Use a private browser or safe device at work/library.

Small, careful steps still count. Quiet help exists.

4) Support services (UK & Scotland)

If you are in immediate danger, call 999 (UK).

  • National Domestic Abuse Helpline (0808 2000 247)
  • Refuge — support for women and children.
  • Men’s Advice Line — support for men.
  • Scottish Women’s Aid
  • Safer.Scot
  • NHS Inform Scotland
  • Childline (0800 1111)
  • Hourglass — support for older people.
  • Shout (text SHOUT to 85258)

5) For friends & family

  • Listen and believe. Avoid “why didn’t you…” questions.
  • Don’t pressure action before they’re ready. Safety planning takes time.
  • Offer specific help: “I can store copies of your documents,” “I can drive on Tuesdays.”
  • Share helplines and check in regularly.

Important Note

The information here is for general understanding and support and is not a substitute for professional advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately.
